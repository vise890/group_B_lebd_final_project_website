Overview

Design

We chose to set our zero energy house in the Urban Setting of South Glasgow. After having analysed the site, the location and the climate, the following step was the building’s design: the distribution of the rooms, the orientation, the position of the necessary windows. Not less important was the choosing of the roof shape to better meet the proper positioning of the PVs. The last step was the choosing of the materials in order to satisfy the PassivHaus standars.

Thermal Modelling and Thermal Demand Reduction

An integrated approach was taken to model the thermal performance of the house. The bulk of the simulation was done with ESP-r. This allowed for an iterative improvement of the building and its components, leading to reduced heating and cooling demands. Data from the other simulations (such as lighting and appliance use) were fed into the model to account for the interconnectedness of all these systems.

Lighting

Aim of the lighting part was to achieve a realistic annual energy demand for our design. Annual demand was based on requirements obtained from references and by using these requirements appropriate models in DiaLUX programs were established. DiaLUX evo program was used to create a model without taking in consideration the daylight. Which means that the artificial lighting needs to provide satisfactory amount of lux to satisfy the requirements. However, it was necessary to obtain information of how often and how long will the artificial lighting have to be in operation. Therefore, DiaLUX 4.11 program was used to take in consideration the daylight. Thereafter, the monthly demand for each room could be obtained from which the total annual demand has been calculated.

Energy Systems

The energy systems are a core part of the building, providing the necessary space heating and hot water demand.  The analysis involved looking at different heat sources and delivery systems that matched the criteria for a net-zero carbon house, and preferably satisfied the PassivHaus standards.  The system chosen would likely rely on the electricity available to the house, so the equipment had to use as little electricity as possible. For this reason, an efficient Ground Source Heat Pump was chosen. To lower heating demand, a Mechanical Ventilation Heat Recovery system was also introduced. An important step was also investigating the appliance use profiles; this served to better quantify total electrical demand and to generate part of the internal gain profiles for the dynamic thermal model of the dwelling.

Water Demand

The water demand part of the website researched a realistic annual consumption of how water which was later used in energy system section of the website.

Energy Generation

When matching the demand side of the energy, there were a few options available that we could adopt. In order to keep the design simple and we adopted for a photo-voltaic system designed to cover our full requirements. The design process required detailed climate data gathering, which was extrapolate through RetScreen4 ; then an appropriate photo-voltaic panel and inverter were selected for the final system analysis. The final annual output was then used in the electrical demand vs supply for further analysis . More detailed informations can be found in the energy generation section of the website.
