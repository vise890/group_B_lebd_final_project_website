Evolution of the thermal model

This section contains the description of how the thermal model evolved. This was done by closely following the development of the geometry, and the progressive improvements in the modelling of the occupancy profiles, appliance demand profiles and lighting profiles.

Each iteration in the thermal model also served as a starting point for the next iteration, by providing  data on how the building was performing at that stage and what were the areas that required most attention.

Note: the reductions and increases in demand figures always refer to the previous iteration.

Iteration #1

The first iteration of the ESP-r thermal model was devised as a simple tool to provide an upper value for the thermal demand of the dwelling. This was a useful starting point for the initial sizing of the in-situ energy generation options and the initial research regarding the energy systems. It was fundamentally aimed at providing a tentative answer to the question: how much energy will the dwelling require?

This iteration was modelled after the first design concept, which was later heavily changed. The model did not account for windows and had no internal heat gains. The construction included PassivHaus compliant insulation in walls and roof (taken from the John Hand development branch of ESP-r). The air tightness levels were also assumed to be compliant with the PassivHaus standard (0.03 AC/hour) and a MVHR system with 90% efficiency was assumed. An unspecified heating and cooling capacity of 2,000 kW was assumed. Heating and cooling set points were configured at 20°C and 24°C respectively. The system controller was assumed to be a basic ON/OFF controller.

To account for the PH standard air tightness and the MVHR system, a simple model for determining an equivalent infiltration rate was used:

I* = I + (1 – E) * V

Where:

  • I* is the equivalent infiltration rate used as input in ESP-r;
  • I is the real infiltration rate (0.03 AC/hours, as per PassivHaus standards);
  • E is the efficiency of the heat exchanger (0.90, assumed);
  • V is the mechanical ventilation rate (0.5 AC/hour, assumed).

Thus:

I* = 0.03 + (1 – 0.90) * 0.5 = 0.08 AC/hour

The results for the initial thermal model of the dwelling were:

  • Heating demand: ~5,000 kWh/year
  • Cooling demand: 0 kWh/year
  • Total demand: ~5,000 kWh/year

No further data was extracted from the model. This initial simulation seemed to be compatible with the climate exploration, in that cooling did not seem to be significant. However, it was noted that, once internal casual gains were added in subsequent iterations, this would change.

Iteration #2

Iteration #1 highlighted the fact that tackling heating would have been a good place to start improving in order to reduce the overall thermal demand of the dwelling. Because of this, Iteration #2 focused on the introduction of passive heating through direct solar gains. Therefore, high quality triple glazing was added to 80% of the South facing facades. A triple glazing construction with argon-filled gaps (U-value 0.89 W/m²K) construction for the glazing was selected from the construction database found in the John Hand branch of ESP-r.

The results from the second iteration were:

  • Heating demand: ~2,500 kWh/year — halved
  • Cooling demand: ~4,000 kWh/year — up from 0
  • Total demand: ~6,500 kWh/year — up by ~1,500 kWh/year

This iteration showed that the heating demand could be halved through direct heat gain. However, it also made clear became apparent that cooling demand could no longer be ignored.

Iteration #3

At this stage, the building geometry evolved in an attempt to provide more available south facing roof area and thus increase the yield of the PV array. Therefore a new thermal model was created with ESP-r. All the strategies investigated in the previous iterations were integrated in this model (PH standard insulation and air tightness, MVHR and direct gains through south facing glazing). The only exception was that the cooling set point was increased to 27°C.

In a further attempt to mitigate overheating, internal thermal mass was added. This was done by employing a 150 mm layer of slate tiles in the floors of the living and bedroom areas.

The results from the third iteration were:

  • Heating demand: ~3,000 kWh/year — up by ~500 kWh/year
  • Cooling demand: ~1,800 kWh/year — down from ~4,000 kWh/year
  • Total demand: ~4,800 kWh/year — down by ~1,700 kWh/year

Note that the new geometry, by having a larger heated and cooled space, lead to an increase in the heating demand. Most of the reduction in cooling demand seemed to come from the new cooling set point at this stage. The thermal mass that was added seemed to have a limited effect on the overall demand; however, since the building volume was increased, the effect of the internal mass could have been significant indeed.

Iteration #4

This iteration investigated the use of overhangs as a mean of avoiding unwanted heat gains during the summer. These would work by preventing some of the solar radiation from entering the house when the sun is high in the sky.

The results from the fourth iteration were:

  • Heating demand: ~3,700 kWh/year — up by ~700 kWh/year
  • Cooling demand: ~300 kWh/year — down by ~1,500 kWh/year
  • Total demand: ~4,000 kWh/year — down by ~800 kWh/year

The iteration showed that the impact of the overhangs was a positive one (a reduction of ~800 kWh/year occurred in overall demand). However, the addition of overhangs also resulted in an increase in heating demand (~700 kWh/year). This was attributed to the overhangs blocking solar radiation during winter, when solar gains were desired. A better solution would have been the adoption of adjustable shading. However, that strategy was not investigated as no convenient way of modelling it was found in ESP-r. This was only due to the inexperience of the author with the software package; apparently adjustable shading can indeed be simulated with ESP-r.

Iteration #5

This iteration was mainly aimed at bringing all the data together, as explained in the data integration section. Internal casual gain profiles were substantially altered at this stage, due to the evolution of their individual models.

Iteration #5 introduced the thermostatic control of ventilation rates to try and reduce the cooling demand. Additionally, as the geometry was further refined, the glazed percentage of the south-exposed facades had to be reduced to 37% in the upper floor and 70% in the ground floor.

The results from the fourth iteration were:

  • Heating demand: ~2,400 kWh/year — down by ~1,300 kWh/year
  • Cooling demand: ~100 kWh/year — down by ~200 kWh/year
  • Total demand: ~2,5000 kWh/year — down by ~1,500 kWh/year

Note that, while the introduction of thermostatically controlled ventilation contributed towards the reduction of cooling demand, there also is a significant reduction in heating. This was attributed to the introduction of much higher appliance, lighting and occupancy internal heat gains, which effectively lowered the need for heating.

Iteration #6

As the comfort analysis was carried out, it became clear that the building overheated in August. In an attempt to mitigate the impact, the set points and rates for the thermostatically controlled ventilation were tweaked.

Thus the final set points used for this iteration were:

  • low: 21°C
  • mid: 22°C
  • high: 25°C

And the ventilation rates were:

  • low: 0.08 AC/hour (windows are closed, MVHR is in operation)
  • mid: 0.5 AC/hour (some window may be open, MVHR bypass valve my be in use)
  • high: 3.0 AC/hour (windows opened, MVHR bypassed, MV rate increased)

The results from this final iteration do not differ greatly from Iteration #5.

Obtaining the thermal model files

The thermal model for iterations 2-6 is available on github. Note that the files as they appear on the site are up to date with Iteration #6. However, since the model was checked into version control since its inception,  with a basic knowledge of git, it is possible to revert the model to all the previous iterations mentioned above. The model can also be downloaded as a zip file.
