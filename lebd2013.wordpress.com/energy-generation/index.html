Energy Generation

Introduction

This page of the website will describe the renewable energy technology; we decided to use in order to cover the Net-zero carbon house energy demand.  It is important to state that we discarded the option of installing a wind turbine in the early stages of the design due to the low effective wind speed and limited space available in the development plot.

Solar energy

Having discarded the use of wind power, the second most harvestable energy available was: Solar energy.  The first step, we took in the designing the appropriate system to covert the solar energy into electricity, was to research the actual feasibility of installing such system given our location. The research of a number of case studies proved that this idea was indeed possible. The following phase was to research the optimum angle using the specified latitude and longitude of the plot. The result was a year average of 34°.

The selected Photo-voltaic panel and inverter

Selecting the appropriate photo-voltaic panel was essential to maximize the solar power harvestable   The group decided to install the Panasonic Sanyo Hit photo-voltaic module with black back sheet. The selection of this product was mainly driven by its outstanding performance. The module has an overall efficiency of 18.6%; however as this efficiency level is achieved under test conditions we felt that taking an actual efficiency of 16% would increase our accurate output values and produce an overall more realistic design, see fig 1.  Also a suitable inverter was selected to invert the DC output current into a AC which can be fed to the grid. Baykee LBK series was chosen for our design; it features an efficiency of 98%, though we included in our calculation a miscellaneous loss of 10%, see fig 2.

The selected inverter

Energy production analysis

The next step in the system design we engaged with, was to choose the right location of the photo-voltaic panels.  As solar energy was our only energy source, it had a major level of priority and impact of the envelope design. Having already confirmed an optimum angle of 34° for the panels, the roof  was also designed to sit at that angle.  Two main geometries were developed at first; these information can be found in the geometry development page.  To decide the best option we started our solar energy production analysis. The solar data gathering of our specif location was done through RETscreen 4, an excel-based energy project analysis tool. We decided to use such tool due to its level of accuracy and realistic data extracted from the NASA Surface Meteorology and Solar Energy Dataset.  As we initially did not know the required amount of photo-voltaic panels required we ran our calculation by assuming full coverage of the roofing area. The comparison between geometry 1 and 2 produced an expected result. Geometry 2 produced more energy due to the smaller roof area being tilted at a 20° rather than being set horizontal like in geometry 1 (including the shading caused by the main roof in the calculation would have increase the energy production difference) ; see fig 3.

The comparison between the energy generated by geometry 1 (red) & 2 (blue)

Following the above results, we decided to go for the 2nd geometry layout.  Further analysis dictated that to cover our annual projected energy demand of 10,000 kWhe/year, consequently we adjusted  the photo-voltaic area to 30m2 and 29m2 tilted at 34° and 20° respectively. However we felt that increasing the main area to 40m2 would produce approximately just over 12, 000 kWhe/year, enough energy to cover the energy demand in case of a poor solar year.
